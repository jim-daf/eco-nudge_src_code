Study Design

Primary design rationale and methodology

Primary

Within-Subjects Design

Every participant performs all 4 tasks sequentially with think-aloud protocol. This eliminates individual differences as a confound, since each participant serves as their own control. It requires fewer participants while maximizing statistical power for eye-tracking metrics across tasks.

Across Iterations

Between-Subjects Element

Design sprints produce multiple interface iterations. Different (or overlapping) participants test each iteration, creating a between-subjects comparison across versions. This lets you measure whether design changes improve usability metrics (SUS) and reduce cognitive load (NASA-TLX) from one sprint to the next.

Qualitative

Think-Aloud Protocol

Concurrent think-aloud method captures real-time reasoning about eco-suggestions, sustainability priorities, and interface comprehension. Audio is recorded alongside eye-tracking data to triangulate gaze patterns with verbal rationale, enabling deeper analysis of RQ1, RQ4, and RQ5.

🔬

Why Within-Subjects?

Eye-tracking metrics vary greatly between individuals (calibration quality, gaze patterns). Within-subjects design controls for these individual differences, making it easier to detect meaningful effects across the 4 tasks with a smaller sample size.

🔄

Why Design Sprints?

Iterative testing through design sprints allows the team to refine the Eco-Nudge interface based on real user data. Each sprint incorporates findings from the previous round, systematically improving usability and eco-nudge effectiveness before the final evaluation.

🗣️

Why Think-Aloud?

Eye tracking tells you where users look, but not why. Think-aloud provides the missing rationale — whether users understand CO₂ data, why they accept/decline suggestions, and how they interpret sustainability settings. Essential for RQ4 and RQ5.

⚠️

Order Effects Mitigation

Since all participants do Task 1→2→3→4, there is a learning effect. This is intentional — the tasks follow a natural user journey. RQ6 specifically measures learnability (TTFF decrease) across tasks. Counterbalancing would break the narrative flow.

🧭

Why Free Exploration First?

Letting users browse the Eco-Nudge site without any task instructions creates a natural baseline. Their unguided scanpaths and TTFF reveal initial discoverability and intuitive navigation patterns — before task instructions bias their attention. This baseline enriches RQ6 and RQ7 analysis.

📊

What It Captures

During free exploration, eye-tracking records which features users naturally gravitate to first, which areas they ignore, and how they mentally model the app's structure. This data is compared against the task-guided scanpaths to measure the gap between intuitive and intended navigation.

⏱️

Duration & Protocol

Approximately 2–3 minutes of unguided exploration. Participants are told: "Take a look around the app and explore anything that interests you. Think out loud about what you see." No specific goals are given. Eye tracking and think-aloud are both active.

🔗

Comparison Baseline

Free-explore gaze data serves as the within-subject baseline for each participant. TTFF during free exploration vs. Task 1 shows how much task framing accelerates feature discovery. Scanpath entropy during free-explore vs. tasks measures how instructions constrain visual search.

Research Variables

Independent, dependent, and confound/control variables

IV 1

Task Type

The specific task a participant is performing. Each task targets different features of the Eco-Nudge interface and maps to specific research questions.

T1: Recipe Analysis & Eco-Negotiation T2: Custom Recipe Input T3: AI-Assisted Meal Planning T4: Impact & Preferences
IV 2

Design Sprint Iteration

The version of the interface being tested. Each design sprint produces a refined iteration based on findings from the previous round of user testing.

Sprint 1 (Baseline) Sprint 2 Sprint 3 Sprint N...
IV 3

Suggestion Intensity Level

The nudge intensity slider in Settings (1–5 scale). Controls how actively the AI suggests ingredient alternatives — from gentle to persistent nudging.

1 — Gentle 2 3 — Balanced 4 5 — Persistent
IV 4

Focus Area Configuration

User-selected sustainability priorities in Settings: carbon footprint reduction, health improvements, and/or cost optimization. Changes which suggestions the system prioritizes.

🌍 Carbon ❤️ Health 💰 Cost Combinations
DV 1

Fixation Duration on Eco-Suggestions

Total and average fixation duration on the Eco Suggestions panel. Longer fixations indicate deliberative evaluation rather than quick accept/decline.

📐 Measured in ms — I-DT algorithm | Mapped to RQ1

DV 2

Fixation Count on CO₂ Data Areas

Number of fixations on the Carbon Score Card and CO₂ savings information. Higher counts suggest users attend to environmental data before deciding.

📐 Count per AOI | Mapped to RQ2

DV 3

Acceptance / Decline Rate

Proportion of eco-swap suggestions accepted vs. declined. Core behavioral outcome tied to how CO₂ presentation and fixation patterns influence decisions.

📐 Ratio (accepted / total offered) | Mapped to RQ1, RQ2

DV 4

Dwell Time: Quick Prompts vs. Free-Text

Time spent gazing at quick AI prompt buttons versus the free-text input area in the AI Assistant. Reveals information-seeking strategy.

📐 Dwell time ratio (ms) | Mapped to RQ3

DV 5

Time to First Fixation (TTFF)

Time from page/view load until the user first fixates on a key AOI. Measures initial discoverability and learnability across tasks.

📐 Measured in ms per AOI | Mapped to RQ6

DV 6

Scanpath Sequences

Ordered sequence of AOI fixations (transition matrices). Reveals whether users follow the intended navigation flow or deviate.

📐 AOI transition matrices, string-edit distance | Mapped to RQ7

DV 7

Pupil Dilation

Change in pupil diameter while evaluating swap suggestions vs. reading recipe details. Proxy for cognitive load during decision-making.

📐 Relative change in mm (if hardware supports) | Mapped to RQ8

DV 8

Task Completion Time

Total time from task start to completion. Indicates efficiency and potential confusion points across the 4 tasks.

📐 Measured in seconds | All Tasks

DV 9

SUS Score (System Usability Scale)

Post-task usability questionnaire (10-item, 0–100 scale). Captures perceived ease of use, learnability, and overall satisfaction. Scores above 68 indicate above-average usability.

📐 Score 0–100 | Usability Goal: Satisfaction & Efficiency

DV 10

NASA-TLX Score

Post-task workload assessment across 6 dimensions: mental demand, physical demand, temporal demand, performance, effort, and frustration. Lower scores indicate lower cognitive load.

📐 Score 0–100 (weighted) | Usability Goal: Efficiency

DV 11

Think-Aloud Qualitative Themes

Coded verbal protocols revealing user reasoning about sustainability priorities, settings exploration, and recipe comprehension. Analyzed via thematic analysis.

📐 Thematic coding (affinity diagramming) | Mapped to RQ4, RQ5

DV 12

Number of Ingredient Swaps Made

Count of eco-friendly ingredient substitutions the user actually applied. Behavioral measure of eco-nudge persuasion effectiveness.

📐 Integer count | Mapped to RQ1, RQ2

CV 1

Calibration Accuracy

WebGazer.js calibration error in pixels. Poor calibration (>100px) degrades all gaze metrics. Validated post-calibration; sessions with unacceptable accuracy should be flagged.

Control — Measured & filtered
CV 2

Screen Size & Resolution

Different display sizes affect AOI pixel coordinates and gaze dispersion. Participants use their own laptops with WebGazer.js.

Confound — Record device specs
CV 3

Ambient Lighting Conditions

Webcam-based eye tracking is sensitive to lighting changes. Glare, backlighting, and dim rooms degrade tracking quality.

Control — Standardize test environment
CV 4

Prior Sustainability Knowledge

Participants with existing eco-awareness may evaluate suggestions differently than those without. Could bias acceptance rates and think-aloud content.

Confound — Pre-study questionnaire
CV 5

Cooking Experience

Experienced cooks may understand ingredient substitutions intuitively, requiring less fixation time on suggestions. Novices may need more deliberation.

Confound — Demographic screening
CV 6

Tech Literacy / Digital Fluency

Familiarity with web apps and chat interfaces affects task completion time and navigation efficiency, independent of the eco-nudge design quality.

Confound — Demographics
CV 7

Fatigue & Learning Effects (Task Order)

Within-subjects sequential tasks introduce learning (positive) and fatigue (negative). Later tasks may show faster TTFF but also more errors due to tiredness.

Control — Fixed order (intentional); track time-per-task
CV 8

Webcam Quality

WebGazer.js accuracy depends on webcam resolution and framerate. Built-in laptop cameras vary significantly (720p vs 1080p, 30fps vs 60fps).

Confound — Record webcam specs; filter by sampling rate
CV 9

Time of Day

Participant alertness varies with time of day. Morning sessions may yield different pupil dilation baselines than evening sessions.

Control — Record session timestamps
CV 10

Recipe Familiarity

In Task 1, the recipe chosen by the participant may vary in familiarity. A well-known recipe requires less ingredient reading, skewing fixation data.

Confound — Note recipe chosen; could assign fixed recipe

Hypotheses — Null (H₀) & Alternative (Hₐ)

Each research question generates a testable null and alternative hypothesis

Directional One-Tailed Hypotheses

Used when prior theory or the app's design rationale predicts a specific direction of the effect (e.g., "longer fixation leads to higher acceptance"). Most of our hypotheses are directional because the Eco-Nudge design embeds deliberate nudge strategies — we can predict which direction the effect should go. One-tailed tests give more statistical power when we're confident in the direction.

Non-Directional Two-Tailed Hypotheses

Used when we predict a difference exists but cannot confidently predict its direction. RQ3 (attention distribution between prompt types) and RQ7 (scanpath alignment) are non-directional because user behavior could plausibly go either way — users might prefer free-text from the start, and scanpath deviations may not always harm usability. Two-tailed tests are more conservative.

H1

Deliberative Evaluation vs. Rapid Decision

RQ1 Task 1 Directional ➜
H₀
There is no significant difference in eco-suggestion acceptance rate between users who exhibit longer fixation durations (>500ms) on suggestion cards and those who make rapid (<300ms) accept/decline decisions.
Hₐ
Users who exhibit longer fixation durations (>500ms) on eco-suggestion cards will have a significantly higher acceptance rate of ingredient swaps than users who make rapid decisions. Deliberation leads to more informed sustainable choices.
H2

CO₂ Visualization Influence

RQ2 Task 1 Directional ➜
H₀
Fixating on the Carbon Score Card and CO₂ savings data before evaluating suggestions has no significant effect on users' likelihood of accepting ingredient substitutions compared to skipping directly to accept/decline buttons.
Hₐ
Users who fixate on the Carbon Score Card and CO₂ savings data before evaluating suggestions will be significantly more likely to accept ingredient substitutions than users who skip directly to accept/decline buttons. Visual CO₂ data primes pro-environmental behavior.
H3

Quick Prompts vs. Free-Text Attention Distribution

RQ3 Task 2 Non-Directional ↔
H₀
There is no significant difference in dwell time distribution between quick AI prompt buttons and the free-text input area, either in initial or follow-up interactions.
Hₐ
The distribution of dwell time between quick AI prompt buttons and free-text input will differ significantly between initial and follow-up interactions — suggesting a shift in information-seeking strategy as users gain familiarity with the AI assistant.
H4

Sustainability Priority Exploration

RQ4 Task 3 Directional ➜
H₀
Users will distribute dwell time equally across Carbon, Health, and Cost focus areas in the Settings view. Think-aloud data will reveal no dominant sustainability priority.
Hₐ
Users will spend significantly more dwell time on Carbon-related focus areas compared to Health or Cost options. Think-aloud data will reveal that environmental impact is the primary motivator for engaging with the app.
H5

Recipe Input & Analysis Comprehension

RQ5 Task 4 Directional ➜
H₀
After submitting a custom recipe for analysis, there is no significant difference in which AOI receives the user's first fixation — Carbon Score Card and ingredient list are equally likely first targets.
Hₐ
After analysis loads, users' first fixation will land on the Carbon Score rather than the ingredient list — indicating that the carbon footprint visualization is the most salient element of the analysis output.
H6

Learnability Across Tasks

RQ6 All Tasks Directional ➜
H₀
Time to First Fixation (TTFF) on key interface elements does not change significantly from the free exploration phase through Task 1 to Task 4.
Hₐ
TTFF on key interface elements will decrease significantly from free exploration through Task 1 to Task 4, indicating that users learn the interface layout and become more efficient at locating relevant features over time.
H7

Scanpath Alignment with Intended Flow

RQ7 All Tasks Non-Directional ↔
H₀
There is no significant correlation between the degree of scanpath alignment with the intended design flow and users' SUS scores or NASA-TLX mental demand ratings.
Hₐ
The degree of scanpath alignment with the intended design flow (e.g., Recipe → Carbon Score → Ingredients → Suggestions → Accept/Decline) is significantly correlated with SUS scores and NASA-TLX mental demand — indicating that navigation efficiency and perceived usability are linked.
H8

Cognitive Load During Swap Evaluation

RQ8 Task 1 Directional ➜
H₀
There is no significant difference in pupil dilation or fixation duration between evaluating eco-swap suggestions and passively reading recipe ingredient details.
Hₐ
Participants will exhibit significantly higher pupil dilation and longer fixation durations when evaluating eco-swap suggestions compared to passively reading recipe details — indicating that ingredient substitution decisions are cognitively demanding.

Usability Goals & Metrics

How SUS and NASA-TLX map to usability goals

📊 SUS — Satisfaction & Learnability

  • Goal: Overall perceived usability ≥ 68 (above average)
  • Satisfaction: Items 1, 3, 5, 7, 9 capture positive sentiment
  • Learnability: Items 4, 10 specifically measure learning curve
  • Benchmark: Compare across design sprint iterations to track improvement
  • Correlation: Map SUS to scanpath alignment (H7) — users with well-aligned scanpaths expected to give higher SUS

🧠 NASA-TLX — Efficiency & Cognitive Load

  • Goal: Weighted workload score ≤ 50 (manageable cognitive demand)
  • Mental Demand: Directly maps to RQ8 (cognitive load of swap evaluation)
  • Temporal Demand: Correlates with task completion time (DV8)
  • Effort: Triangulate with fixation count and dwell time patterns
  • Frustration: Cross-reference with declined suggestions and think-aloud negative sentiment

⚡ Efficiency (Eye-Tracking Derived)

  • Task Completion Time: Should decrease across iterations (design sprints)
  • TTFF Improvement: Faster discovery of key elements = better information architecture
  • Scanpath Efficiency: Fewer AOI transitions to complete a task = more efficient layout
  • Fixation-to-Action Ratio: Lower ratio means less visual search before acting
